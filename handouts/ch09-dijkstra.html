Chapter 9 — Graphs II: Weighted Shortest Paths & Dijkstra’s Algorithm

CSS 343 — Data Structures, Algorithms & Discrete Mathematics II. Course text, primary reading for Lecture 9.
Edges now carry weights, and “shortest” means least total weight rather than fewest edges. The one operation every shortest-path algorithm is built from (edge relaxation), Dijkstra’s greedy ordering of it, a proof that the greedy choice is correct when weights are nonnegative, the priority-queue implementation that makes it \(O(E\log V)\), and the exact line of the proof that negative weights break. With worked traces, the correctness proof, links to the interactive demos, and exercises.

Chapter 8 gave us breadth-first search, which finds the path with the fewest edges and proves it — a proof that worked precisely because every edge counted the same, one step. This chapter lets edges carry weights: a distance, a time, a price, a latency. Now the cheapest route is a matter of the total weight along it, and the fewest-edge path and the least-weight path can be different paths entirely, so BFS answers the wrong question. The fix, Dijkstra’s algorithm, turns out to be BFS with one part swapped — the plain queue becomes a priority queue keyed by distance — which is why we built heaps back in Chapter 6. And this time we do not just watch it work in a demo; we prove it.

1. Weighted graphs and the shortest-path problem

Definition (weighted graph, path cost) A weighted graph attaches to each edge a number, its weight \(w(u,v)\). The cost of a path is the sum of the weights of its edges. A shortest path from \(s\) to \(t\) is one of minimum cost; its cost is the distance \(\delta(s,t)\), written \(\delta(t)\) when the source is fixed.

A weight is whatever the edges model — miles on a road, dollars for a flight, milliseconds of latency. On the graph below, the path \(0 \to 3 \to 4 \to 5\) costs \(6 + 2 + 3 = 11\); the path \(0 \to 1 \to 2\) costs \(4 + 1 = 5\). The shortest-path problem asks, among possibly very many routes, for the one with the smallest such sum.

Fewest edges is not least weight

The whole reason this chapter exists is that weighting the edges divorces two notions that Chapter 8 held together. On this little digraph — the lecture’s running example — 

  0 —2→ 1
  |      |
  5      4        plus one direct edge   0 —9→ 3
  ↓      ↓
  2 —8→ 3

three routes lead from \(0\) to \(3\): the direct edge (one hop, cost \(9\)), the detour \(0 \to 1 \to 3\) (two hops, cost \(2 + 4 = 6\)), and \(0 \to 2 \to 3\) (two hops, cost \(5 + 8 = 13\)). The fewest-edge route is the direct edge; the least-weight route is the two-hop detour through \(1\), at \(6\). BFS minimizes the number of edges and would seize the \(9\)-cost direct edge — the wrong answer. We need an algorithm that sums weights, and it must somehow discover that a longer-looking, more-hops path can be cheaper. That hops-beats-direct shape recurs throughout the chapter.

Definition (single-source shortest paths) Given a weighted digraph and a source \(s\), the single-source shortest-paths (SSSP) problem asks for \(\delta(v)\) for every vertex \(v\), together with enough information to reconstruct an actual shortest path to each.

We solve the all-destinations version because the single-pair version — one \(s\), one \(t\) — is, surprisingly, no easier in general: the only known way to be sure \(t\)’s distance is final is essentially to settle every vertex nearer than \(t\) first, which is what Dijkstra does anyway. The one saving is that we may stop the moment \(t\) is settled (§3).

Why not enumerate? Exponentially many paths — a proof

The obvious brute force — list every \(s \to t\) path, keep the cheapest — founders on a counting fact. Take the little digraph above, drop its direct edge, and chain \(k\) copies of the diamond end to end, the exit vertex of each serving as the entrance of the next: 

        a1        a2        a3
      ↗    ↘    ↗    ↘    ↗    ↘
   v0        v1        v2        v3     k = 3 diamonds
      ↘    ↗    ↘    ↗    ↘    ↗
        b1        b2        b3
Proposition (exponentially many paths) For every \(k \ge 1\), the chain of \(k\) diamonds is a digraph with \(V = 3k+1\) vertices and \(E = 4k\) edges on which the number of distinct \(v_0 \to v_k\) paths is exactly \(2^k = 2^{(V-1)/3}\) — exponential in \(V\).
Proof (product rule). Formally, the vertices are \(v_0, \dots, v_k\) together with \(a_i, b_i\) for \(1 \le i \le k\), and the edges are \(v_{i-1} \to a_i\), \(v_{i-1} \to b_i\), \(a_i \to v_i\), \(b_i \to v_i\). Every \(v_0 \to v_k\) path visits \(v_1, v_2, \dots, v_k\) in order: the only edges leaving \(v_{i-1}\) go to \(a_i\) or \(b_i\), and the only edge leaving either of those goes to \(v_i\). So between consecutive \(v\)’s a path makes exactly one free choice — top (\(a_i\)) or bottom (\(b_i\)) — and a path is a sequence of \(k\) independent binary choices. Different choice sequences give different paths (they differ at the first diamond where the choices part), and every sequence is realizable. By the product rule the paths number \(\underbrace{2 \cdot 2 \cdots 2}_{k} = 2^k\). ∎

Exponential means gone almost immediately. \(k = 30\) diamonds is a graph of \(91\) vertices holding \(2^{30} \approx 10^9\) routes — a billion paths in a graph that fits on this page. \(k = 100\) (\(301\) vertices, \(400\) edges) packs \(2^{100} \approx 1.3 \times 10^{30}\) routes; enumerating a billion per second, the survey finishes in roughly \(4 \times 10^{13}\) years — three thousand times the age of the universe. Two remarks. The chain is a DAG, so acyclicity is no escape. And grids are just as bad: moving only right and up, an \(n \times n\) grid has \(\binom{2n}{n} \approx 4^n / \sqrt{\pi n}\) corner-to-corner routes. Dijkstra will nevertheless compute every true distance in \(O(E \log V)\), without listing a single path — the structural property that makes such a shortcut possible at all is next.

The shortest-paths tree and optimal substructure

Brute force is hopeless — the diamond chain just proved it. What saves us is that shortest paths nest inside one another.

Proposition (optimal substructure) Every sub-path of a shortest path is itself a shortest path. If \(s \rightsquigarrow u \rightsquigarrow t\) is a shortest \(s\)-to-\(t\) path, then its prefix \(s \rightsquigarrow u\) is a shortest \(s\)-to-\(u\) path.
Proof (exchange argument). Suppose the prefix \(s \rightsquigarrow u\) were not shortest, so some route \(s \rightsquigarrow u\) is strictly cheaper. Splice that cheaper prefix in front of the original suffix \(u \rightsquigarrow t\): the result is an \(s\)-to-\(t\) path of strictly smaller total cost than the one we called shortest — a contradiction. (The splice is a valid path because the suffix still starts at \(u\).) ∎

Optimal substructure is exactly what lets us build shortest paths out of shorter ones instead of enumerating them — extend a known-shortest path by one relaxed edge — and it is the same property that will power dynamic programming in Chapter 14. One consequence is structural: the shortest paths from \(s\) overlap into a shortest-paths tree rooted at \(s\), because each vertex \(v\) has a single best predecessor on its shortest path. Storing that one parent[v] pointer per vertex — \(\Theta(V)\) space — is enough to reconstruct every shortest path, exactly as BFS’s tree edges did for unit weights.

An undirected weighted edge \(u - v\) is simply the two directed edges \(u \to v\) and \(v \to u\) with the same weight; Dijkstra only ever reads out-edges, so it never needs to know which kind it started with. Road networks (two-way streets) are the common undirected case.

The nonnegative-weight assumption

All weights must be \(\ge 0\) Dijkstra’s algorithm requires every edge weight to be nonnegative. Physical costs — distances, times, prices, capacities — are naturally \(\ge 0\), so it fits most real problems. Negative weights break the greedy argument, and §5 points at the exact line of the correctness proof that fails and the algorithm (Bellman–Ford) that replaces it. State this precondition up front: it is the one thing Dijkstra cannot be talked out of.

2. Edge relaxation: the one operation

Every shortest-path algorithm in this chapter — and beyond it — is built from a single primitive applied in some order. Learn the primitive and its invariant first; the algorithms are then just orderings.

Keep a tentative distance dist[v] for every vertex: an over-estimate of \(\delta(v)\) that we drive downward as we learn more. 

  dist[s] = 0        // zero cost to reach the source from itself
  dist[v] = ∞        // every other vertex: no route known yet
Definition (edge relaxation) To relax an edge \(u \to v\) of weight \(w\) is to ask whether routing through \(u\) beats \(v\)’s current best, and if so, to take it: 
  if (dist[u] + w < dist[v]) {
      dist[v] = dist[u] + w;   // found a cheaper route to v
      parent[v] = u;           // remember the edge, for the path
  }
Example (one relaxation) Suppose \(\texttt{dist}[u] = 3\), \(\texttt{dist}[v] = 10\), and the edge \(u \to v\) has weight \(4\). Then \(\texttt{dist}[u] + w = 3 + 4 = 7 < 10\), so the edge relaxes: \(\texttt{dist}[v] \leftarrow 7\) and \(\texttt{parent}[v] \leftarrow u\). If later some other route offers \(\texttt{dist}[v] = 6\), it relaxes again to \(6\). The value only ever falls.

Relaxation is monotone — it never raises a label — and it is also honest: a relaxed label always equals the cost of some real path. That honesty is the invariant the whole subject rests on.

Invariant (labels never lie low) At all times, either \(\texttt{dist}[v] = \infty\) or \(\texttt{dist}[v]\) is the cost of some genuine \(s \to v\) path. In particular \(\texttt{dist}[v] \ge \delta(v)\) always, and when no edge can be relaxed, \(\texttt{dist}[v] = \delta(v)\) for every \(v\).
Proof. Initially \(\texttt{dist}[s] = 0\) (the empty path) and all others are \(\infty\); the claim holds. A relaxation of \(u \to v\) sets \(\texttt{dist}[v] = \texttt{dist}[u] + w\), and by induction \(\texttt{dist}[u]\) is the cost of a real \(s \to u\) path, so appending the edge \(u \to v\) exhibits a real \(s \to v\) path of exactly that cost; the invariant is preserved, and since a real path cannot cost less than the shortest, \(\texttt{dist}[v] \ge \delta(v)\). For the terminal claim, suppose no edge relaxes yet some \(\texttt{dist}[v] > \delta(v)\). Take a shortest \(s \to v\) path and let \(x \to y\) be its first edge with \(\texttt{dist}[x] = \delta(x)\) but \(\texttt{dist}[y] > \delta(y)\) (the source has \(\texttt{dist}[s] = \delta(s) = 0\), and \(v\) fails, so such an edge exists along the path). Then \(\texttt{dist}[x] + w(x,y) = \delta(x) + w(x,y) = \delta(y) < \texttt{dist}[y]\), so \(x \to y\) can be relaxed — contradicting “no edge relaxes.” ∎

This is the backbone of correctness for the entire family. Every algorithm below reaches the terminal “nothing relaxes” state; they differ only in how efficiently, that is, in the order they relax edges.

A bad order forces the same edge to relax many times (Bellman–Ford does \(V-1\) full passes because it never knows which labels are final); the right order relaxes each edge once. On a DAG that order is free — relax in topological order (Chapter 8’s product) and each vertex is final before its out-edges are touched, giving linear-time shortest paths even with negative weights. Dijkstra finds a once-each order for general nonnegative graphs, and the rest of the chapter is about it.

Finally, relaxation is also what reconstructs paths. Each improving relaxation records parent[v]; to recover a route, walk \(t \to \texttt{parent}[t] \to \texttt{parent}[\texttt{parent}[t]] \to \cdots \to s\) and reverse it. Distances tell you how far; parents tell you which way.

3. Dijkstra’s algorithm

Dijkstra’s algorithm chooses the relaxation order greedily. Maintain a set of settled vertices, whose shortest distances are final. Repeatedly take the unsettled vertex with the smallest tentative distance, settle it, and relax its out-edges. 

  settled : shortest distance is now KNOWN (final)
  frontier: discovered, tentative, waiting in the priority queue
  ∞       : not yet discovered

Each iteration promotes the nearest frontier vertex into settled and pushes its improved neighbors onto the frontier — exactly BFS promoting the front of its queue, except “nearest” is now by total weight, not edge count. That single swap (queue \(\to\) priority queue) is the whole difference from BFS. 

// Lazy Dijkstra: a min-heap of (dist, vertex) pairs. Settle a vertex the moment
// it POPS as the minimum; skip stale pops; relax its out-edges and push any
// improved neighbor. Needs all weights >= 0.
vector<long> dijkstra(WGraph& g, int s) {
    vector<long> dist(g.V, LONG_MAX);      // dist[v] = best-known cost s->v
    vector<bool> settled(g.V, false);      // settled[v] = dist[v] is final
    priority_queue<pair<long,int>, vector<pair<long,int>>,
                   greater<>> pq;              // min-heap keyed by distance
    dist[s] = 0;
    pq.push({0, s});
    while (!pq.empty()) {
        int u = pq.top().second; pq.pop();     // nearest unsettled
        if (settled[u]) continue;              // stale entry — skip
        settled[u] = true;                     // dist[u] is now FINAL
        for (Edge e : g.adj[u])
            if (!settled[e.to] && dist[u] + e.w < dist[e.to]) {
                dist[e.to] = dist[u] + e.w;     // relax: a cheaper route to e.to
                pq.push({dist[e.to], e.to});    // push, don't decrease-key
            }
    }
    return dist;
}
// Lazy Dijkstra: a min-heap of {dist, vertex}. Settle a vertex the moment it
// POPS as the minimum; skip stale pops; relax and push improved neighbors.
long[] dijkstra(WGraph g, int s) {
    long[] dist = new long[g.V];         // dist[v] = best-known cost s->v
    Arrays.fill(dist, Long.MAX_VALUE);   // MAX_VALUE plays the role of infinity
    boolean[] settled = new boolean[g.V]; // settled[v] = dist[v] is final
    // min-heap of {dist, vertex}, ordered by distance
    PriorityQueue<long[]> pq =
        new PriorityQueue<>((a, b) -> Long.compare(a[0], b[0]));
    dist[s] = 0;
    pq.add(new long[]{0, s});
    while (!pq.isEmpty()) {
        int u = (int) pq.poll()[1];          // nearest unsettled
        if (settled[u]) continue;            // stale entry — skip
        settled[u] = true;                   // dist[u] is now FINAL
        for (Edge e : g.adj.get(u))
            if (!settled[e.to] && dist[u] + e.w < dist[e.to]) {
                dist[e.to] = dist[u] + e.w;   // relax: a cheaper route to e.to
                pq.add(new long[]{dist[e.to], e.to});
            }
    }
    return dist;
}

# Lazy Dijkstra: a min-heap of (dist, vertex). Settle a vertex the moment it
# POPS as the minimum; skip stale pops; relax and push improved neighbors.
def dijkstra(g, s):
    dist = [float("inf")] * g.V         # dist[v] = best-known cost s->v
    settled = [False] * g.V              # settled[v] = dist[v] is final
    dist[s] = 0
    pq = [(0, s)]                            # min-heap of (dist, vertex)
    while pq:
        d, u = heapq.heappop(pq)             # nearest unsettled
        if settled[u]:                       # stale entry — skip
            continue
        settled[u] = True                    # dist[u] is now FINAL
        for v, w in g.adj[u]:
            if not settled[v] and dist[u] + w < dist[v]:
                dist[v] = dist[u] + w        # relax: a cheaper route to v
                heapq.heappush(pq, (dist[v], v))
    return dist

use std::cmp::Reverse;

// Lazy Dijkstra. BinaryHeap is a MAX-heap, so wrap each (dist, vertex) in
// Reverse to pop the SMALLEST distance. Settle on pop; skip stale pops.
fn dijkstra(g: &WGraph, s: usize) -> Vec<i64> {
    let n = g.adj.len();
    let mut dist = vec![i64::MAX; n];    // dist[v] = best-known cost s->v
    let mut settled = vec![false; n];    // settled[v] = dist[v] is final
    let mut pq = BinaryHeap::new();
    dist[s] = 0;
    pq.push(Reverse((0i64, s)));
    while let Some(Reverse((_d, u))) = pq.pop() {   // nearest unsettled
        if settled[u] { continue; }                 // stale entry — skip
        settled[u] = true;                          // dist[u] is now FINAL
        for &(v, w) in &g.adj[u] {
            if !settled[v] && dist[u] + w < dist[v] {
                dist[v] = dist[u] + w;              // relax: cheaper route to v
                pq.push(Reverse((dist[v], v)));
            }
        }
    }
    dist
}

Two details in that loop carry the algorithm. First, a vertex is settled when it pops as the minimum, not when it is first discovered — settling on discovery would freeze a label that a later, cheaper route could still lower, the classic Dijkstra bug. Second, an improved neighbor is handled by pushing a new entry rather than editing the old one; the stale duplicate is discarded when it later pops. We return to both.

Example (a full trace) On the small graph \(0\to1(2)\), \(0\to2(5)\), \(1\to2(1)\), \(1\to3(7)\), \(2\to3(3)\): 
  settle 0 (dist 0):  relax 0→1 ⇒ dist[1]=2;  0→2 ⇒ dist[2]=5
  settle 1 (dist 2):  relax 1→2: 2+1=3 < 5 ⇒ dist[2]=3;
                      relax 1→3: 2+7=9      ⇒ dist[3]=9
  settle 2 (dist 3):  relax 2→3: 3+3=6 < 9 ⇒ dist[3]=6
  settle 3 (dist 6):  done
  distances:  0  2  3  6
Watch vertex \(2\): its label dropped from \(5\) to \(3\) via the \(1 \to 2\) edge before it settled — relaxation finding a better route just in time. Because \(2\) settles (at \(3\)) before \(3\) does, the improvement to \(\texttt{dist}[3]\) is already in hand when \(3\) settles.

The priority queue, and lazy deletion

The bottleneck is “which unsettled vertex is nearest?” A linear scan answers it in \(O(V)\); a min-heap (Chapter 6) answers it in \(O(\log V)\). This is the payoff for building heaps first — the heap is the engine that makes Dijkstra fast. In C++ a priority_queue<pair<long,int>, …, greater<>> is a min-heap of \(\{\text{distance},\text{vertex}\}\) pairs, which compare lexicographically — distance first — exactly as wanted.

A textbook heap cannot cheaply lower the key of an entry already inside it, so instead of a decrease-key we simply push another entry with the smaller distance and let the outdated one go stale. When a stale entry finally pops, its vertex is already settled, and the if (settled[u]) continue; line discards it. This lazy deletion is simpler than maintaining an indexed heap, and it costs only a constant factor:

Cost

Proposition (Dijkstra cost) With a binary-heap priority queue, lazy Dijkstra runs in \(O(E\log V)\) on a graph with \(V\) vertices and \(E\) edges.
Proof. Each improving relaxation pushes exactly one entry, and only an edge can cause a relaxation, so there are at most \(E\) pushes and hence at most \(E\) pops over the whole run. Each push and pop is \(O(\log(\text{heap size}))\), and the heap holds at most \(E \le V^2\) entries, so \(\log(\text{heap size}) \le \log V^2 = 2\log V = O(\log V)\). Settling a vertex scans its out-edge list, and each list is scanned once (a vertex settles once), for \(\sum_u \deg(u) = E\) edge examinations in total. Adding it up: \(O(E\log V)\) for the heap traffic plus \(O(V+E)\) for the scans, i.e. \(O(E\log V)\) on a connected graph (\(E \ge V-1\)). ∎

This is the same “sum of degrees is \(E\)” accounting as Chapter 8’s \(\Theta(V+E)\), now with a \(\log V\) price for each priority-queue touch. There is also a queue-free variant:

The array-scan version, \(O(V^2)\) Drop the heap and, each round, scan all vertices for the nearest unsettled one: \(V\) rounds, an \(O(V)\) scan each, \(O(V^2)\) total. It is simpler and — for a dense graph, where \(E = \Theta(V^2)\) — actually faster, since \(V^2 < E\log V\) there. The heap version wins on sparse graphs, which is most of them. This is the same dense-versus-sparse choice that picked the adjacency matrix over the list in Chapter 8.
Sharper heaps: the \(O(E + V\log V)\) bound The \(\log V\) factor rides on every priority-queue operation, and a Fibonacci heap (Cormen et al.) shaves it where it hurts. It supports decrease-key in \(O(1)\) amortized and extract-min in \(O(\log V)\) amortized, so eager Dijkstra does \(E\) decrease-keys and \(V\) extract-mins for a total of \(O(E + V\log V)\) — asymptotically better on dense graphs (where \(E\) approaches \(V^2\)). The constant factors are large enough that a plain binary heap wins in practice, but the bound is the theoretical state of the art, and it is why decrease-key is worth naming even though our lazy code avoids it.

4. Why Dijkstra is correct

The greedy claim is that the nearest unsettled vertex can be settled — its tentative distance is already final. Everything rides on it, and it is exactly here that nonnegativity does its work.

Theorem (greedy is safe) When all weights are \(\ge 0\), the moment Dijkstra pops a vertex \(u\) as the nearest unsettled vertex, \(\texttt{dist}[u] = \delta(u)\).
Proof. Let \(S\) be the settled set, and assume inductively that \(\texttt{dist}[x] = \delta(x)\) for every \(x \in S\) (true at the start, when \(S = \{s\}\) and \(\texttt{dist}[s] = 0\)). We show no path to \(u\) can cost less than \(\texttt{dist}[u]\); since \(\texttt{dist}[u]\) is the cost of a real path (the invariant of §2), that forces \(\texttt{dist}[u] = \delta(u)\).
Take any path \(P\) from \(s\) to \(u\). It begins inside \(S\) (at \(s\)) and ends outside \(S\) (at \(u\), which is about to be settled), so it has a first edge \(x \to y\) that leaves \(S\): \(x \in S\), \(y \notin S\). Split \(P\) at that edge and bound each piece: \[ \operatorname{cost}(P) = \underbrace{\operatorname{cost}(s \rightsquigarrow x)}_{\ge\ \delta(x)} + w(x,y) + \underbrace{\operatorname{cost}(y \rightsquigarrow u)}_{\ge\ 0} \;\ge\; \delta(x) + w(x,y). \] The tail is \(\ge 0\) because every weight is nonnegative — this is the one and only place the assumption is used. Now \(x\) is settled, so \(\delta(x) = \texttt{dist}[x]\), and when \(x\) settled we relaxed \(x \to y\), giving \(\texttt{dist}[y] \le \texttt{dist}[x] + w(x,y) = \delta(x) + w(x,y)\). Finally \(u\) was popped as the nearest unsettled vertex and \(y\) is also unsettled, so \(\texttt{dist}[u] \le \texttt{dist}[y]\). Chaining, \[ \operatorname{cost}(P) \;\ge\; \delta(x) + w(x,y) \;\ge\; \texttt{dist}[y] \;\ge\; \texttt{dist}[u]. \] Every path to \(u\) costs at least \(\texttt{dist}[u]\), so \(\delta(u) \ge \texttt{dist}[u]\); with \(\texttt{dist}[u] \ge \delta(u)\) from the invariant, \(\texttt{dist}[u] = \delta(u)\), and the induction extends to \(S \cup \{u\}\). ∎

The theorem also explains the speed. Because a settled distance is provably final, no vertex is ever settled twice and no out-edge is ever relaxed twice — one pass suffices, in contrast to Bellman–Ford’s \(V-1\) blind passes. Correctness of the greedy step is precisely what buys the single pass. It also justifies the early exit: if you only need \(\delta(t)\), stop the instant \(t\) settles — its label will never change — often long before the whole graph is explored. That early exit is the doorway to A* (§6).

The relaxation framework behind the proof Cormen et al. isolate the three facts every relaxation-based proof leans on, worth naming because they are reused for Bellman–Ford and the DAG algorithm too. The upper-bound property: once \(\texttt{dist}[v]\) reaches \(\delta(v)\) it never changes, and it is always \(\ge \delta(v)\) (our invariant of §2). The convergence property: if \(s \rightsquigarrow u \to v\) is a shortest path and \(\texttt{dist}[u] = \delta(u)\) at the moment \(u \to v\) is relaxed, then \(\texttt{dist}[v] = \delta(v)\) forever after. The path-relaxation property: if the edges of a shortest \(s\rightsquigarrow v\) path are relaxed in path order (possibly with other relaxations interleaved), then \(\texttt{dist}[v] = \delta(v)\). Every shortest-path algorithm is a scheme for relaxing each shortest path’s edges in order: Dijkstra by settling nearest-first, Bellman–Ford by brute-force rounds, the DAG algorithm by topological order. Correctness is always “did we relax the right edges in the right order?”

5. Negative weights and the limits of greed

The proof used nonnegativity in exactly one place: the tail \(\operatorname{cost}(y \rightsquigarrow u) \ge 0\). Remove that guarantee and a farther boundary crossing can undercut a label that was already settled — and Dijkstra never reconsiders a settled vertex.

Example (Dijkstra settles too early) 
  s —1→ a         edges:  s→a (1),  s→b (2),  b→a (−4)
  |        ↑
  2       −4        greedy from s: settle s(0); nearest is a(1) —
  |        |           declared FINAL. Then settle b(2); relax b→a:
  ⇒ b ———'           2 + (−4) = −2 < 1, but a is already settled.
Vertex \(a\) settles at distance \(1\), but the true shortest route \(s \to b \to a\) costs \(2 + (-4) = -2\). The cheaper route arrives after \(a\) is frozen, so Dijkstra silently returns \(1\), not \(-2\). Nothing crashes — the answer is just wrong — which is what makes the precondition matter more than most.
Interactive Dijkstra demo — append the edge 7 1 -20 to the running graph and rebuild, then run from \(0\). Dijkstra happily reports \(\texttt{dist}[1] = 4\), but the demo’s final edge re-check — a scan for any edge that still relaxes, which by the invariant of §2 should find none — flags \(7 \to 1\) in red, because \(15 + (-20) = -5 < 4\). The red edge is the proof’s failed line made visible. (Worse: that edge closes the cycle \(1 \to 3 \to 4 \to 7 \to 1\) of weight \(5+2+7-20 = -6\), so a shortest path to \(1\) does not even exist — see below.) The re-check is a fine debugging trick for your own implementation: at the end, nothing should relax.

Bellman–Ford, and negative cycles

When weights may be negative, drop the greedy shortcut and relax blindly: pass over all \(E\) edges, \(V-1\) times. 

  for i in 1 .. V-1:
      for each edge (u, v, w):
          relax(u, v, w)

A shortest path has at most \(V-1\) edges, and one can show that after round \(i\) every shortest path using at most \(i\) edges is final; so \(V-1\) rounds settle everything, at a cost of \(O(VE)\). This is Bellman–Ford, and it handles negative edges that Dijkstra cannot.

Negative cycles have no shortest path If some cycle’s weights sum to a negative number, “shortest path” becomes meaningless: each extra lap lowers the cost, so the infimum is \(-\infty\). Bellman–Ford detects this with one more pass — after \(V-1\) rounds all labels must be final, so if a \(V\)-th pass still relaxes an edge, a negative cycle is reachable. Notice this is the same “does anything still relax?” test the demo runs; there it exposed a broken greedy choice, here it exposes a broken problem.
Aside: longest paths are hard Shortest and longest sound symmetric, but they are worlds apart. The longest simple path problem is NP-hard (it would solve Hamiltonian path), and the natural trick — negate every weight and run a shortest-path algorithm — fails, because negating a positive cycle makes it negative, landing in the undefined territory above. A small change to a problem can flip it from linear-time to intractable.

6. One frontier, many searches

Chapter 8 ended on a template: an explore loop whose behavior is set by the container holding the frontier. Dijkstra is the next row of that table, and seeing it there is the point of these two chapters.

frontier containerremoves nextyou get
stack (LIFO)deepestDFS
queue (FIFO)oldestBFS (fewest edges)
priority queue by \(\texttt{dist}[u]\)nearestDijkstra
priority queue by \(\texttt{dist}[u] + h(u)\)most promisingA*

BFS is literally Dijkstra with all weights equal to \(1\): its queue already dequeues vertices in distance order (the queue-monotone lemma proved in Chapter 8), so it is a priority queue for unit weights. Weighted edges are the only reason the frontier must become a real heap.

Dijkstra and Prim are the same skeleton Prim’s minimum-spanning-tree algorithm (Chapter 11) also grows a tree by pulling the nearest frontier vertex from a priority queue. The two differ only in the key: Dijkstra prioritizes \(\texttt{dist}[u] + w\), the total distance from the source; Prim prioritizes \(w\) alone, the single cheapest edge connecting a vertex to the growing tree. Same greedy, PQ-driven growth; different priority. Recognizing the shared skeleton makes both easier to hold in mind.

A*: the same relaxation, a smarter order

Plain Dijkstra explores outward in all directions equally. For a single target, A* biases the priority by a heuristic \(h(u)\) estimating the cost still to go, so it expands toward the goal first: 

  priority = dist[u] + h(u)          // h(u) ≈ remaining cost to the target

What is \(h\), and how is it computed? Not from the graph — knowing the true remaining cost is the very problem being solved. It comes from cheap domain knowledge stored on the vertices. On a road map every vertex carries coordinates, so \(h(u) = \sqrt{(x_u - x_t)^2 + (y_u - y_t)^2}\) — the straight-line distance to the target, one \(O(1)\) formula, no graph search; on a game board, Manhattan distance plays the same role. This is why A* lives on embedded graphs (maps, grids, game worlds): an abstract graph offers no side-information to build an \(h\) from. The implementation is one changed line of §3’s code: push \(\{\texttt{dist}[v] + h(v),\, v\}\) instead of \(\{\texttt{dist}[v],\, v\}\).

It stays exact as long as \(h\) is admissible — never overestimates the true remaining cost (straight-line distance is admissible for roads, since a road is never shorter than the straight line). An admissible heuristic only reorders the exploration; it never misses the shortest path. Set \(h \equiv 0\) and A* degenerates to Dijkstra, a clean sanity check. This is what real map routers build on — along with searching from both ends and precomputed shortcuts — but every layer sits on the same settle-and-relax core.

Ask in order: weighted at all? a DAG (then topological-order relaxation is linear and handles negatives too)? nonnegative? sparse or dense? BFS and heap-Dijkstra are the everyday tools; the rest are specialists. All of them — and the GPS routers, OSPF network routing, transit planners, and game pathfinders that use them — are the same idea: relax edges until nothing improves, in the cleverest order you can afford.

7. Exercises (with solutions)

  1. In the diamond chain of §1, keep each diamond’s direct edge \(v_{i-1} \to v_i\) as well (so each diamond looks exactly like the running example). How many distinct \(v_0 \to v_k\) paths are there now? How many vertices does the graph need before the path count passes \(10^{30}\)?
    solution Each stage now offers three ways from \(v_{i-1}\) to \(v_i\): via \(a_i\), via \(b_i\), or the direct edge. The choices remain independent, so by the product rule there are \(3^k\) paths. For the count: \(3^k > 10^{30}\) needs \(k > 30/\log_{10} 3 \approx 62.9\), so \(k = 63\) diamonds — \(V = 3k+1 = 190\) vertices — already exceed \(10^{30}\) paths (versus \(301\) vertices for the \(2^k\) version: the fatter branching factor gets there sooner).
  2. On the graph \(0\to1(1)\), \(0\to2(4)\), \(1\to2(1)\), \(1\to3(5)\), \(2\to3(1)\), run Dijkstra from \(0\). Give the settle order and the final distances.
    solution Settle \(0(0)\): relax \(\texttt{dist}[1]=1\), \(\texttt{dist}[2]=4\). Settle \(1(1)\) (nearest): \(1\to2\) gives \(1+1=2 < 4\) so \(\texttt{dist}[2]=2\); \(1\to3\) gives \(6\) so \(\texttt{dist}[3]=6\). Settle \(2(2)\): \(2\to3\) gives \(2+1=3 < 6\) so \(\texttt{dist}[3]=3\). Settle \(3(3)\). Order \(0,1,2,3\); distances \(0,1,2,3\). Note \(2\)’s label fell from \(4\) to \(2\) and \(3\)’s from \(6\) to \(3\) before each settled — the two-hop detour beating the direct edge, twice.
  3. A classmate marks a vertex settled the moment they push it onto the priority queue, rather than when it pops. Give a three-vertex weighted graph on which this produces a wrong distance, and name the true distance.
    solution Take \(0\to1(4)\), \(0\to2(1)\), \(2\to1(2)\). Pushing settles \(1\) at \(4\) the instant \(0\)’s edges are scanned. But the real shortest route is \(0\to2\to1 = 1+2 = 3\); since \(1\) is frozen at \(4\), the \(2\to1\) relaxation is ignored and the reported \(\texttt{dist}[1]=4\) is wrong (true distance \(3\)). Settling only on pop keeps \(1\) improvable until \(2\) has relaxed into it.
  4. Explain why lazy Dijkstra’s priority queue can hold up to \(E\) entries yet the running time is still \(O(E\log V)\), not \(O(E\log E)\).
    solution Each improving relaxation pushes one entry and there are at most \(E\) of them, so the heap holds \(O(E)\) entries and each operation is \(O(\log E)\). But \(E \le V^2\), so \(\log E \le \log V^2 = 2\log V\), which is \(O(\log V)\). The extra duplicates from lazy deletion cost only this constant factor, so the bound is \(O(E\log V)\) — identical in order to the eager, decrease-key version whose heap never exceeds \(V\).
  5. Point to the exact step of the correctness proof that fails when a single negative-weight edge is present, and connect it to the settle-too-early example.
    solution The step \(\operatorname{cost}(y \rightsquigarrow u) \ge 0\), which bounds the tail of the path after it first leaves the settled set. With a negative edge in that tail, a path crossing the boundary at a farther vertex \(y\) can still reach \(u\) more cheaply than \(\texttt{dist}[u]\), so “nearest boundary crossing wins” is no longer valid. In the example \(s\to a(1)\), \(s\to b(2)\), \(b\to a(-4)\), vertex \(a\) settles at \(1\) but \(s\to b\to a = -2\); the \(-4\) tail undercuts the settled label, exactly the failed inequality.
  6. Give a weighted graph where the shortest \(s\)-to-\(t\) path has more edges than every other \(s\)-to-\(t\) path, and state what BFS would return instead.
    solution \(s\to t(10)\) directly, and \(s\to a(1)\), \(a\to b(1)\), \(b\to t(1)\). The shortest path \(s\to a\to b\to t\) has three edges and cost \(3\); the only other path is the single edge \(s\to t\) of cost \(10\). BFS minimizes edge count and returns the one-edge path, reporting distance \(10\) instead of the true \(3\). This is the fewest-edge-versus-least-weight gap of §1, made as wide as you like.
  7. You must answer many point-to-point queries on one fixed road network with nonnegative weights. Which parts of Dijkstra can you reuse across queries, and what does the early-exit rule save on a single query?
    solution The graph (the weighted adjacency list) is reused across all queries; the per-query state is \(\texttt{dist}\), \(\texttt{settled}\), the parent pointers, and the heap, all reset each time. For one query \(s \to t\), the correctness theorem says \(t\)’s label is final the moment \(t\) pops, so you may stop then rather than settling the rest of the graph — often a large saving. Steering the search toward \(t\) with an admissible heuristic (A*) prunes even more, without losing exactness.
  8. On a DAG, shortest paths can be computed in \(\Theta(V+E)\) with no priority queue, even with negative weights. Explain the order of relaxation that achieves this and why it works.
    solution Topologically sort the DAG (Chapter 8), then relax every vertex’s out-edges in that order. When a vertex \(u\) is processed, every edge into \(u\) comes from a vertex earlier in topological order, which has already been processed, so \(\texttt{dist}[u]\) is already final — no later relaxation can improve it. Each edge relaxes exactly once, giving \(\Theta(V+E)\). Nonnegativity is not needed because the topological order, not a greedy distance argument, guarantees finality; there are simply no back edges to reconsider.
  9. True or false, with justification: “Adding a constant \(c > 0\) to every edge weight leaves all shortest paths unchanged.”
    solution False. Adding \(c\) to every edge adds \(c \cdot (\text{number of edges})\) to a path’s cost, so it penalizes paths with more edges. A two-edge path costing \(2\) becomes \(2 + 2c\); a one-edge path costing \(9\) becomes \(9 + c\); for \(c > 7\) the formerly-expensive direct edge now wins. The transformation changes which path is shortest, so it cannot be used to, say, turn negative weights nonnegative and run Dijkstra — a common and wrong idea.
