You sorted in CSS 342; this chapter goes deeper, and the frame is divide-and-conquer — split a problem into smaller subproblems, solve them recursively, and combine the answers. The master theorem reads the cost of such an algorithm straight off its recurrence, no unrolling required. Mergesort and quicksort are the two famous sorts you get by putting the work in the combine step versus the divide step, and both reach \(\Theta(n\log n)\); a lower-bound argument then proves that no algorithm that sorts by comparing keys can beat them. Finally, quicksort’s partition, used once more, gives quickselect, which finds a single order statistic — the median, say — in linear time, faster than sorting.

1. Divide-and-conquer and the master theorem

Definition (divide-and-conquer) A divide-and-conquer algorithm solves a problem in three steps, applied recursively: divide it into smaller subproblems, conquer each by recursing, and combine the sub-answers into the full answer. A base case — a subproblem small enough to solve directly — stops the recursion.

You have already met the paradigm: binary search halves a sorted array (\(T(n) = T(n/2) + O(1)\)), and mergesort splits and merges. The base case is easy to overlook and fatal to omit — a size-\(0\)-or-\(1\) array is already sorted, an empty search range means the key is absent — and forgetting it means infinite recursion. The cost of a divide-and-conquer algorithm obeys a recurrence \[ T(n) = a\,T(n/b) + f(n), \] where \(a\) is the number of subproblems, \(b\) the factor by which each shrinks, and \(f(n)\) the cost of dividing and combining. Mergesort’s is \(T(n) = 2\,T(n/2) + \Theta(n)\).

The master theorem

Theorem (master theorem) For \(T(n) = a\,T(n/b) + f(n)\) with \(a \ge 1\), \(b > 1\), compare \(f(n)\) with \(n^{\log_b a}\):
  1. if \(f(n) = O(n^{\log_b a - \varepsilon})\) for some \(\varepsilon > 0\) (leaf work dominates), then \(T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_b a})\);
  2. if \(f(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_b a})\) (the two tie), then \(T(n) = \Theta(n^{\log_b a}\log n)\);
  3. if \(f(n) = \Omega(n^{\log_b a + \varepsilon})\) and a regularity condition holds (combine work dominates), then \(T(n) = \Theta(f(n))\).
Why the three cases (a recursion-tree sketch). Sum the tree level by level. The root costs \(f(n)\); level \(i\) has \(a^i\) subproblems of size \(n/b^i\), costing \(a^i f(n/b^i)\); the bottom level has \(a^{\log_b n} = n^{\log_b a}\) leaves, each \(\Theta(1)\). The total is the geometric-like series \(\sum_i a^i f(n/b^i)\), and, like any geometric series, it is dominated by its largest term: the root if the terms shrink (case 3, cost \(\Theta(f(n))\)), the leaves if they grow (case 1, cost \(\Theta(n^{\log_b a})\)), and if every term is equal, one level’s cost times the \(\Theta(\log n)\) depth (case 2). The three cases are the three behaviors of that series. ∎

The critical exponent is \(\log_b a\) — the leaf count — and the whole method is: compute it, compare to \(f(n)\), pick the case.

Example (reading recurrences) 
  mergesort:      2T(n/2) + Θ(n)   n^(log₂2) = n = f  → case 2  ⇒ Θ(n log n)
  binary search:   T(n/2) + Θ(1)   n^(log₂1) = 1 = f  → case 2  ⇒ Θ(log n)
  case 1 example: 4T(n/2) + Θ(n)   n^(log₂4) = n² > n  → case 1  ⇒ Θ(n²)
  Karatsuba mult: 3T(n/2) + Θ(n)   n^(log₂3) ≈ n^1.58 > n  → Θ(n^1.58)
  case 3 example: 2T(n/2) + Θ(n²)  n^(log₂2) = n < n²  → case 3  ⇒ Θ(n²)
Karatsuba multiplication does three half-size multiplies instead of four, and its \(n^{1.58}\) beats grade-school \(n^2\) — a divide-and-conquer win read straight off the recurrence.
Balance is everything The savings depend on balanced splits. Even splits give \(\log n\) levels and \(\Theta(n\log n)\); a skewed split into sizes \(1\) and \(n-1\) gives \(n\) levels and \(\Theta(n^2)\). And the combine must be cheap: halving a quadratic pass and recursing on that alone still sums to \(\Theta(n^2)\), because a top-heavy geometric series is dominated by its root. The \(n\log n\) sweet spot needs both balanced splits and a linear combine — exactly \(2T(n/2) + \Theta(n)\). This is the difference between mergesort (always splits at the midpoint) and quicksort (splits at a pivot).

Divide-and-conquer reaches far beyond sorting: binary search (\(O(\log n)\)), Karatsuba and Strassen’s matrix multiplication (\(7T(n/2)+\Theta(n^2) = \Theta(n^{2.81})\)), the closest-pair-of-points algorithm (\(\Theta(n\log n)\)), and the FFT are all recurrences the master theorem reads at a glance. The two sorts below are its most famous instances.

When the master theorem does not apply The master theorem covers \(a\,T(n/b) + f(n)\), but real recurrences wander outside that form, and Cormen et al. give three tools for the rest. The substitution method: guess the answer and prove it by induction (the honest way to verify a bound the tree method suggested). The recursion-tree method: draw the tree, sum each level, and add up the geometric series — exactly the sketch behind the three cases, and the way to handle a gap case like \(T(n) = 2T(n/2) + n\log n\) (which is \(\Theta(n\log^2 n)\), a factor the basic theorem cannot name). And the Akra–Bazzi method, a heavier generalization that handles unequal subproblem sizes — \(T(n) = T(n/5) + T(7n/10) + \Theta(n)\), the median-of-medians recurrence of §5, whose solution \(\Theta(n)\) no elementary case delivers. The recurrence is still the analysis; the master theorem is just the common special case.

2. Mergesort

Mergesort is the “easy divide, hard combine” instance: cut the array in half (trivial), sort each half recursively, and merge the two sorted halves (the work). Because the split is always at the midpoint, the recursion is perfectly balanced and the \(\Theta(n\log n)\) is guaranteed on every input.

Example (the merge) Merge two sorted runs by repeatedly taking the smaller front element: 
  L: 2 5 8    R: 1 3 9    out: [ ]
  1 < 2 → out 1        L: 2 5 8   R: 3 9
  2 < 3 → out 2        L: 5 8     R: 3 9
  3 < 5 → out 3        L: 5 8     R: 9
  5 < 9 → out 5;  8 < 9 → out 8;  R tail 9 → out 9
  → 1 2 3 5 8 9
Each element is copied exactly once, so the merge is \(\Theta(n)\). The subtlety is the tail: when one run empties, append the rest of the other. Note that the merge cannot be done in place cheaply — it writes into a \(\Theta(n)\) auxiliary buffer, then copies back — which is mergesort’s one real drawback. 
// Merge two adjacent sorted runs a[lo..mid] and a[mid+1..hi] into sorted order,
// using aux[] as scratch. Every element is copied once: Theta(n).
void merge(int a[], int lo, int mid, int hi, int aux[]) {
    for (int k = lo; k <= hi; k++) aux[k] = a[k];    // copy into scratch
    int i = lo, j = mid + 1;
    for (int k = lo; k <= hi; k++) {
        if (i > mid)              a[k] = aux[j++];    // left run exhausted
        else if (j > hi)          a[k] = aux[i++];    // right run exhausted
        else if (aux[j] < aux[i]) a[k] = aux[j++];    // take the smaller front
        else                      a[k] = aux[i++];    // ties take left: STABLE
    }
}

// Top-down mergesort: sort each half, then merge. T(n) = 2T(n/2) + Theta(n).
void mergesort(int a[], int lo, int hi, int aux[]) {
    if (hi <= lo) return;                 // base case: 0 or 1 element
    int mid = lo + (hi - lo) / 2;
    mergesort(a, lo, mid, aux);           // sort the left half
    mergesort(a, mid + 1, hi, aux);       // sort the right half
    merge(a, lo, mid, hi, aux);           // combine
}

# scratch. Every element is copied once: Theta(n).
def merge(a, lo, mid, hi, aux):
    aux[lo:hi + 1] = a[lo:hi + 1]         # copy into scratch
    i, j = lo, mid + 1
    for k in range(lo, hi + 1):
        if i > mid:                       # left run exhausted
            a[k] = aux[j]
            j += 1
        elif j > hi:                      # right run exhausted
            a[k] = aux[i]
            i += 1
        elif aux[j] < aux[i]:             # take the smaller front (ties: left)
            a[k] = aux[j]
            j += 1
        else:                             # ties take left: STABLE
            a[k] = aux[i]
            i += 1

def mergesort(a, lo, hi, aux):
    if hi <= lo:                          # base case: 0 or 1 element
        return
    mid = (lo + hi) // 2
    mergesort(a, lo, mid, aux)            # sort the left half
    mergesort(a, mid + 1, hi, aux)        # sort the right half
    merge(a, lo, mid, hi, aux)            # combine
// Merge sorted runs a[lo..=mid] and a[mid+1..=hi] using aux as scratch. O(n).
fn merge(a: &mut [i32], lo: usize, mid: usize, hi: usize, aux: &mut [i32]) {
    aux[lo..=hi].copy_from_slice(&a[lo..=hi]);       // copy into scratch
    let (mut i, mut j) = (lo, mid + 1);
    for k in lo..=hi {
        if i > mid {                                 // left run exhausted
            a[k] = aux[j]; j += 1;
        } else if j > hi {                           // right run exhausted
            a[k] = aux[i]; i += 1;
        } else if aux[j] < aux[i] {                  // take the smaller front
            a[k] = aux[j]; j += 1;
        } else {                                     // ties take left: STABLE
            a[k] = aux[i]; i += 1;
        }
    }
}

    if hi <= lo { return; }               // base case: 0 or 1 element
    let mid = lo + (hi - lo) / 2;
    mergesort(a, lo, mid, aux);           // sort the left half
    mergesort(a, mid + 1, hi, aux);       // sort the right half

The recurrence \(T(n) = 2T(n/2) + \Theta(n)\) is master-theorem case 2, and the recursion tree shows why directly:

Top-down or bottom-up; and stability The recursion above is top-down. The same merges can run bottom-up: merge adjacent runs of size \(1\), then \(2\), then \(4\), … — iterative, no call stack, same \(\Theta(n\log n)\). Either way, mergesort is stable (equal keys keep their input order, because the merge breaks ties toward the left run), which matters when sorting records by a secondary key and then stably by a primary key. Its profile: \(\Theta(n\log n)\) time on every input, \(\Theta(n)\) auxiliary space, stable — the guaranteed, stable, but not-in-place sort.

3. Quicksort

Quicksort is mergesort’s mirror image: the divide does the work and the combine is free. It partitions the array around a chosen pivot so that smaller elements come before it and larger after — leaving the pivot in its final sorted position — then recurses on each side. Once both sides are sorted, the whole array is sorted, in place, with no merge buffer.

Example (Lomuto partition, pivot = last) Partition \([3, 7, 1, 8, 2, 5]\) with pivot \(5\). The index \(i\) marks the boundary of the “\(< 5\)” region; whenever an element beats the pivot, grow the region and swap the element in: 
  [ 3 7 1 8 2 | 5 ]      pivot 5,  i = −1
  3 < 5 → i=0, swap a[0],a[0]  → 3 7 1 8 2
  7 < 5 ? no
  1 < 5 → i=1, swap a[1],a[2]  → 3 1 7 8 2
  8 < 5 ? no
  2 < 5 → i=2, swap a[2],a[4]  → 3 1 2 8 7
  place pivot: swap a[3],a[5]     → 3 1 2 [5] 7 8
The pivot \(5\) lands at index \(3\), its final sorted position; everything left is \(< 5\), everything right is \(> 5\), though neither side is yet sorted. That single linear pass is what quicksort recurses around.

The same partition as an array, cell by cell. The green prefix is the “\(< \text{pivot}\)” region (indices \(0\ldots i\)); the outlined cell is the one the scan index \(j\) is examining; the shaded cell is the pivot. Each element that beats the pivot grows the green region and swaps in. 

// Lomuto partition around the last element as pivot. Elements < pivot are moved
// to the front; the pivot ends in its final sorted slot, whose index is returned.
int partition(int a[], int lo, int hi) {
    int pivot = a[hi];
    int i = lo - 1;                       // boundary of the "< pivot" region
    for (int j = lo; j < hi; j++)
        if (a[j] < pivot)
            swap(a[++i], a[j]);           // grow the region, swap the element in
    swap(a[i + 1], a[hi]);                // drop the pivot just past the region
    return i + 1;                         // the pivot's final index
}

// Quicksort: partition, then recurse on each side. No combine step.
void quicksort(int a[], int lo, int hi) {
    if (hi <= lo) return;                 // base case: 0 or 1 element
    int p = partition(a, lo, hi);         // pivot lands at its final index p
    quicksort(a, lo, p - 1);              // sort the smaller side
    quicksort(a, p + 1, hi);              // sort the larger side
}
// Lomuto partition around the last element as pivot. Elements < pivot are moved
// to the front; the pivot ends in its final sorted slot, whose index is returned.
static int partition(int[] a, int lo, int hi) {
    int pivot = a[hi];
    int i = lo - 1;                       // boundary of the "< pivot" region
    for (int j = lo; j < hi; j++)
        if (a[j] < pivot) {
            i++;
            int t = a[i]; a[i] = a[j]; a[j] = t;    // swap the element in
        }
    int t = a[i + 1]; a[i + 1] = a[hi]; a[hi] = t;  // drop the pivot into place
    return i + 1;                         // the pivot's final index
}

# to the front; the pivot ends in its final sorted slot, whose index is returned.
def partition(a, lo, hi):
    pivot = a[hi]
    i = lo - 1                            # boundary of the "< pivot" region
    for j in range(lo, hi):
        if a[j] < pivot:
            i += 1
            a[i], a[j] = a[j], a[i]       # swap the element into the region
    a[i + 1], a[hi] = a[hi], a[i + 1]     # drop the pivot into its final slot
    return i + 1                          # the pivot's final index

        return

    quicksort(a, lo, p - 1)               # sort the smaller side
    quicksort(a, p + 1, hi)               # sort the larger side
// Lomuto partition around the last element as pivot. Elements < pivot are moved
// to the front; the pivot ends in its final sorted slot, whose index is returned.
fn partition(a: &mut [i32], lo: usize, hi: usize) -> usize {
    let pivot = a[hi];
    let mut i = lo;                       // next free slot of the "< pivot" region
    for j in lo..hi {
        if a[j] < pivot {
            a.swap(i, j);                 // swap the element into the region
            i += 1;
        }
    }
    a.swap(i, hi);                        // drop the pivot into its final slot
    i                                     // the pivot's final index
}

// Quicksort: partition, then recurse on each side. No combine step.
fn quicksort(a: &mut [i32], lo: usize, hi: usize) {
    if hi <= lo { return; }               // base case: 0 or 1 element
    let p = partition(a, lo, hi);         // pivot lands at its final index p
    if p > lo { quicksort(a, lo, p - 1); }   // guard: usize cannot go below 0
    quicksort(a, p + 1, hi);              // sort the larger side
}

The worst case, and how to defeat it

Quicksort’s balance depends on the pivot, and a bad pivot is disastrous.

Proposition (quicksort cost) With balanced pivots quicksort obeys \(T(n) = 2T(n/2) + \Theta(n) = \Theta(n\log n)\). With a pivot that always peels off one element — \(T(n) = T(n-1) + \Theta(n)\) — it degrades to \(\Theta(n^2)\).

The quadratic case is not exotic: a fixed last-element pivot on already-sorted input picks the maximum every time, peeling one element per level for \(n\) levels — exactly \(n-1 + n-2 + \cdots + 1 = \binom{n}{2}\) comparisons. The fix is the pivot:

  • a first or last element pivot is \(\Theta(n^2)\) on sorted data — never use it there;
  • a random pivot (or shuffling first) gives expected \(\Theta(n\log n)\) on every input — the \(\Theta(n^2)\) event now depends on coin flips, not the data, so an adversary who knows your code still cannot force it;
  • median-of-three (first, middle, last) is a cheap heuristic that dodges the sorted-input trap in practice.
Proposition (expected comparisons, CLRS) Randomized quicksort makes \(2(n+1)H_n - 4n \approx 2n\ln n \approx 1.39\,n\log_2 n\) comparisons in expectation, where \(H_n\) is the \(n\)-th harmonic number.
Sketch (indicator random variables). Let \(z_1 < z_2 < \cdots < z_n\) be the elements in sorted order. Two elements are compared at most once during the whole sort, and \(z_i\) and \(z_j\) are compared exactly when the first pivot chosen from the range \(\{z_i, \dots, z_j\}\) is \(z_i\) or \(z_j\) themselves — if any middle element is picked first, it splits them into different subarrays and they never meet. Among the \(j - i + 1\) equally likely first-pivots in that range, two are favorable, so \(\Pr[z_i \text{ compared with } z_j] = \tfrac{2}{j-i+1}\). By linearity of expectation the total is \[ \sum_{i<j}\frac{2}{j-i+1} = \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\sum_{k=1}^{n-i}\frac{2}{k+1} = O(n\log n), \] the inner sum being a harmonic number \(\approx \ln n\). ∎

So quicksort averages about \(39\%\) more comparisons than the \(n\log_2 n\) information-theoretic floor of §4 — yet it is the fastest sort in practice, because each comparison and swap is so cheap and cache-friendly that its small constant beats mergesort’s larger one. Asymptotics tie; the constant, and the memory traffic behind it, decide.

Duplicates and 3-way partitioning Plain two-way quicksort is quadratic on an array of many equal keys (all-equal input is a worst case). Splitting into three regions — less than, equal to, and greater than the pivot — parks every equal key in the middle, done, so recursion touches only the strictly-smaller and strictly-larger parts. This makes quicksort robust on real data with repeated keys.

4. The lower bound: why comparison sorts stop at \(n\log n\)

Mergesort and quicksort both reach \(\Theta(n\log n)\); no algorithm that sorts by comparing keys does asymptotically better, and the reason is a counting argument about information, not about any particular algorithm.

Theorem (comparison-sort lower bound) Any sorting algorithm that only compares keys makes \(\Omega(n\log n)\) comparisons in the worst case.
Proof (decision tree). A comparison sort on \(n\) elements is a binary decision tree: each internal node is a comparison with two outcomes, and each leaf is one of the possible sorted outputs. To sort correctly the tree must have a leaf for each of the \(n!\) possible input orderings, so it has at least \(n!\) leaves. A binary tree of height \(h\) has at most \(2^h\) leaves, so \(2^h \ge n!\), giving \[ h \;\ge\; \log_2(n!) \;=\; \Theta(n\log n) \] by Stirling’s approximation (\(\log_2(n!) = n\log_2 n - n\log_2 e + O(\log n)\)). The height \(h\) is the worst-case number of comparisons, so every comparison sort makes \(\Omega(n\log n)\) of them. ∎

So mergesort and quicksort are asymptotically optimal among comparison sorts — a rare case where we can prove a problem’s difficulty, not just an algorithm’s cost. The escape hatch is to stop comparing:

Non-comparison sorts If the keys have exploitable structure, you can sort in linear time. Counting sort tallies how many keys take each value and is \(\Theta(n + k)\) for keys in a range of size \(k\); radix sort sorts fixed-width keys digit by digit in \(\Theta(d\,n)\) for \(d\) digits. Neither ever compares two keys, so the \(\Omega(n\log n)\) bound simply does not apply to them. The price is that the keys must be small integers or fixed-width strings, and extra space is needed; when the data fits, these are unbeatable.

5. Selection: order statistics in linear time

Often you do not need the whole sorted order, only one order statistic — the \(k\)-th smallest element. \(k = 0\) is the minimum, \(k = n-1\) the maximum, \(k = n/2\) the median, \(k = 0.95n\) the 95th percentile. Sorting to extract one of them, at \(\Theta(n\log n)\), is wasteful. Quickselect does it in linear average time by reusing quicksort’s partition but recursing into only one side. 

// Quickselect: the k-th smallest element (0-indexed). Partition, then recurse
// into ONLY the side that contains rank k. Average Theta(n). Reuses partition().
int quickselect(int a[], int lo, int hi, int k) {
    if (lo == hi) return a[lo];           // one element left
    int p = partition(a, lo, hi);         // pivot lands at rank p
    if (k == p)      return a[p];          // found it
    else if (k < p)  return quickselect(a, lo, p - 1, k);   // left side only
    else             return quickselect(a, p + 1, hi, k);   // right side only
}

    if lo == hi:                          # one element left
        return a[lo]
    p = partition(a, lo, hi)              # pivot lands at rank p
    if k == p:
        return a[p]                       # found it
    elif k < p:
        return quickselect(a, lo, p - 1, k)    # recurse into the left side only
    else:
        return quickselect(a, p + 1, hi, k)    # recurse into the right side only
// Quickselect: the k-th smallest element (0-indexed). Partition, then recurse
// into ONLY the side that contains rank k. Average O(n). Reuses partition().
fn quickselect(a: &mut [i32], lo: usize, hi: usize, k: usize) -> i32 {
    if lo == hi { return a[lo]; }         // one element left
    let p = partition(a, lo, hi);         // pivot lands at rank p
    if k == p { a[p] }                    // found it
    else if k < p { quickselect(a, lo, p - 1, k) }   // left side only
    else { quickselect(a, p + 1, hi, k) }            // right side only
}

After partitioning, the pivot sits at some final rank \(p\). If \(p = k\), the pivot is the answer; if \(k < p\), the \(k\)-th smallest is in the left part; otherwise it is in the right part. Only that one side is searched.

Proposition (quickselect cost) Quickselect runs in \(\Theta(n)\) average time (\(\Theta(n^2)\) worst case with unlucky pivots, removed by randomization).
Sketch. Each call partitions in \(\Theta(n)\) and recurses on one side. A random pivot leaves, on average, at most a constant fraction of the elements — at most \(3/4\) of them — so the surviving size shrinks geometrically, and the total work is bounded by a geometric series \(c\,(n + \tfrac34 n + (\tfrac34)^2 n + \cdots) = \Theta(n)\). The worst case, where every pivot peels one element, is \(\Theta(n^2)\), but a random pivot makes that astronomically unlikely; median-of-medians (below) removes it entirely. ∎
Example (find the median) In \([7, 2, 9, 4, 1, 6, 3]\) (\(7\) elements) the median has rank \(3\) (three below, three above). quickselect(3) partitions, follows only the side containing rank \(3\), and returns \(4\) (the sorted order is \(1\ 2\ 3\ 4\ 6\ 7\ 9\)) in about \(n\) comparisons on average, not \(n\log n\). In C++, std::nth_element(v.begin(), v.begin()+3, v.end()) does exactly this.
Guaranteeing \(O(n)\): median-of-medians To kill the quadratic worst case deterministically, choose a provably good pivot: split the array into groups of \(5\), take each group’s median, then recursively take the median of those medians as the pivot. It guarantees at least a \(30\)/\(70\) split, hence worst-case \(O(n)\) selection (the BFPRT algorithm). The constant is large, so randomized quickselect wins in practice; median-of-medians matters when you need a hard guarantee.
Interactive Quickselect demo. Choose a rank \(k\) and watch half the array get discarded at each partition — that one-sided recursion is what makes it \(\Theta(n)\) rather than \(\Theta(n\log n)\). Compare the counters against a full quicksort: selection does a fraction of the work.

6. Choosing a sort in practice

The two great sorts are duals — mergesort works in the combine, quicksort in the divide — and each wins where the other is weak.

mergesortquicksort
dividetrivial (split)partition (the work)
combinemerge (the work)trivial (in place)
time\(\Theta(n\log n)\) always\(\Theta(n\log n)\) avg, \(\Theta(n^2)\) worst
space\(\Theta(n)\) buffer\(\Theta(\log n)\) stack (expected)
stableyesno

Their asymptotics tie, so the constant factor decides, and quicksort’s is smaller: it sorts in place (no buffer to allocate or pollute the cache), and its partition is a contiguous scan the prefetcher loves. That is why the C++ default std::sort is quicksort-based, while mergesort is chosen when you need a guaranteed \(\Theta(n\log n)\), stability, or are sorting a linked list (merge needs no random access) or external data too big for memory. Real library sorts are hybrids that get the best of each:

  • introsort (std::sort) runs quicksort, falls back to heapsort if the recursion goes too deep (guaranteeing \(\Theta(n\log n)\)), and uses insertion sort on tiny subarrays where its low overhead wins;
  • Timsort (Python’s sort, Java’s object sort) is a mergesort that detects existing sorted runs; it is stable and very fast on partly-sorted real data.

There is no single best sort — the choice depends on \(n\), the key type, and whether you need stability or a worst-case guarantee, the same “match the tool to the requirement” reasoning as the hash-table-versus-balanced-tree decision earlier in the course. And the whole night is one paradigm read by one theorem: every algorithm here is “split, recurse, combine,” and its cost falls straight out of its recurrence — quickselect’s single recursive call (\(T(n/2)+\Theta(n)\), not \(2T(n/2)+\Theta(n)\)) being exactly why it is linear rather than \(n\log n\).

7. Exercises (with solutions)

  1. Use the master theorem on \(T(n) = 9T(n/3) + \Theta(n^2)\) and on \(T(n) = T(n/2) + \Theta(n)\).
    solution First: \(\log_3 9 = 2\), so \(n^{\log_b a} = n^2 = f(n)\) — case 2, giving \(\Theta(n^2\log n)\). Second: \(\log_2 1 = 0\), so \(n^{\log_b a} = 1\) while \(f(n) = n\) dominates — case 3, giving \(\Theta(n)\). (The second is the recurrence for the one-sided scan in quickselect: one subproblem of half size plus a linear partition, hence linear overall.)
  2. Partition \([5, 9, 3, 8, 6, 7]\) with the Lomuto scheme and pivot \(7\) (the last element). Show the array after placement, the pivot’s final index, and the two sides.
    solution \(5 < 7\) (\(i{=}0\)); \(9\) no; \(3 < 7\) swap \(\to 5\ 3\ 9\ 8\ 6\); \(8\) no; \(6 < 7\) swap \(\to 5\ 3\ 6\ 8\ 9\); place pivot by swapping index \(3\) with the last \(\to [5, 3, 6, 7, 9, 8]\). The pivot \(7\) lands at index \(3\); the left side is \(5\ 3\ 6\) (all \(< 7\)) and the right is \(9\ 8\) (all \(> 7\)); neither side is yet sorted.
  3. Exactly how many comparisons does quicksort with a last-element pivot make on the already-sorted input \([1, 2, 3, \dots, n]\), and why?
    solution The last element is always the maximum, so each partition places it at the top and leaves a sorted subarray of size \(n-1\) — peeling one element per level. Partitioning a size-\(m\) subarray does \(m-1\) comparisons, so the total is \((n-1) + (n-2) + \cdots + 1 = \binom{n}{2} = \Theta(n^2)\). This is the worst case; a random pivot or an initial shuffle makes it expected \(\Theta(n\log n)\) instead.
  4. Why is mergesort stable while quicksort (as written here) is not? Give a three-element input where a naive quicksort reorders equal keys.
    solution Mergesort’s merge, on a tie, takes from the left run first, so equal keys keep their original relative order — stability. Lomuto partition swaps distant elements, which can reorder equal keys. Label equal keys to see it: on \([2_a,\ 2_b,\ 1]\) with pivot \(1\) (last), the scan finds nothing \(< 1\), then swaps the pivot into index \(0\), giving \([1,\ 2_b,\ 2_a]\) — \(2_a\) and \(2_b\) have swapped order, so the sort is not stable.
  5. A comparison sort claims to sort any \(8\)-element array in at most \(10\) comparisons. Show this is impossible.
    solution An \(8\)-element sort must distinguish \(8! = 40320\) orderings, so its decision tree needs at least \(40320\) leaves. A binary tree of height \(10\) has at most \(2^{10} = 1024\) leaves — far fewer than \(40320\) — so some orderings share a leaf and are sorted wrongly. The minimum possible worst case is \(\lceil \log_2 8! \rceil = \lceil 15.3 \rceil = 16\) comparisons, so no comparison sort can promise \(10\).
  6. You must report the \(90\)th-percentile latency from \(10^7\) recorded request times. Contrast sorting with selection, and give the running time of the better approach.
    solution Sorting all \(10^7\) values to index the percentile costs \(\Theta(n\log n)\) and produces far more order than needed. Selection answers the single query directly: quickselect for rank \(\lfloor 0.9\,n \rfloor\) runs in \(\Theta(n)\) average time (C++ std::nth_element). For one percentile, selection is the right primitive; only if you need many percentiles at once does a single sort start to pay off.
  7. You are sorting one million \(32\)-bit integers and can spare extra memory. Can you beat \(\Theta(n\log n)\)? If so, name the method and its cost; if not, explain why.
    solution Yes — the \(\Omega(n\log n)\) bound applies only to comparison sorts, and these keys have structure. Radix sort in a few passes over fixed-width digits (say four \(8\)-bit passes) is \(\Theta(d\,n)\) with \(d = 4\), i.e. linear in \(n\), using counting sort within each pass. It never compares two keys, so the lower bound does not apply. The cost is the extra \(\Theta(n + k)\) space per pass, which the problem allows.
  8. True or false, with justification: “Divide-and-conquer always beats a direct \(\Theta(n^2)\) algorithm.”
    solution False. Splitting helps only when the subproblems are balanced and the combine is cheap. The recurrence \(T(n) = 2T(n/2) + \Theta(n^2)\) (a quadratic combine) is master-theorem case 3 and stays \(\Theta(n^2)\); an unbalanced split \(T(n) = T(n-1) + \Theta(n)\) is also \(\Theta(n^2)\). Only balanced splits with a cheap (e.g. linear) combine reach the \(\Theta(n\log n)\) sweet spot, as mergesort does — splitting alone is not magic.
